Competition

David A. Wheeler

April 22, 2004

Microsoft allowed its Internet Explorer browser to languish for years; after all, once it had most of the market, it had no reason to improve it. But in 2004 the browser wars recommenced, due to combination of serious vulnerabilities, lacking functionality, and - most importantly - renewed competition (primarily from OSS/FS). Internet News soon reported that "Faced with a new kind of browser war, Microsoft has kick-started renewed development around its flagship Internet Explorer (IE), prompting speculation that IE 7.0 could be coming down the pike."

Interview with Xiph's Monty "The mp3 patents cover a number of audio coding techniques that were obvious to me in my first weeks of coding study and effectively hobble any credible competition from using mp3 to compete with Thomson. Four years ago I was employed by an internet radio company that was refused an mp3 patent by Thomson (we asked for the right to use mp3 for one year; they set the price for the patent licenses at $60 million) because we were competing directly with a company partially owned by Thomson. Thomson used these patents not for just compensation, but to eliminate a competitor. We had to start completely from scratch with Ogg because the mp3 patents prevented us from standing (and building) on the shoulders of giants. We had real ideas to offer, but couldn't afford to ante up."
