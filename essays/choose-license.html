How Governments/Organizations Should Choose a Free-Libre / Open Source Software (FLOSS, FOSS, or OSS/FS) License

by David A. Wheeler

2008-01-26

This essay helps individuals, companies, governments, and other organizations choose a free-libre / open source software (FLOSS) license. If it's a modification to an existing project, and you're releasing the modification back to the FLOSS project, you should normally release the modification using that project's license (at least). If it's a new project, where possible, you should only choose one of these licenses: MIT, BSD-new, LGPL, or GPL (and maybe Apache 2.0). Understand their differences, and then make your selection for each project based on your goals for that project.

Introduction

There are now a vast number of Free-Libre / open source software (FLOSS, FOSS, or OSS/FS) programs; billions of dollars of effort have gone into their development. In other documents I show that there are many quantitative studies justifying FLOSS use and that FLOSS programs are commercial programs.

But if you choose to develop FLOSS software, you will need to license it somehow. Problem is, there are dozens of FLOSS licenses... so which license do you choose?

The answer is usually easy if you're making a minor modification to an existing FLOSS program, and want to submit the change back to the FLOSS project: you have to (at least) release the modification using that FLOSS program's existing license. This often includes creating plug-ins or general-purpose programming language libraries. There are good reasons to submit a change back to the main FLOSS project: if you submit it, and it's a real improvement, the change is is likely to be included in future versions of the program. If that happens, you don't bear the substantial burden of maintenance (where nearly all software development costs are, as documented by Dr. Barry Boehm and others). Thus, when you get a later version of the program, you get the benefit of the improvements you made (and presumably needed), along with improvements made by many others. Usually the existing FLOSS project requires you to use their license as-is, so there isn't really a decision between licenses - it's a decision on whether or not using one particular licensing model is acceptable for your component. An organization will typically need to review the release for various legal reasons to make sure they aren't giving away crown jewels, but that's it. You might want to also release the software under another more popular license, if the existing FLOSS program uses a relatively non-standard one, in which case this document can still help you.

But if you're starting a new project (including a major new subproject), or for some other reason you have a license choice, you must choose a license... and this can get complicated in a hurry. This can be even more complicated for organizations like businesses and governments, because there may be many people who have to agree on the decision. There are dozens of existing licenses that you could choose, and of course there are many other decisions if you choose to write your own license.

Yet it turns out that this is really much easier in most cases than people make it. Generally you should (1) only consider a very short list of mutually-compatible licenses that are widely used (as described next), (2) gain more understanding of those few licenses, and (3) pick one of them based on your goals for that particular project.

Consider a Very Short List of Licenses

Fundamentally, FLOSS development approaches work because they enable people to work together to build more capable or higher-quality works than they could (for the same amount of effort) if they did not release the software under a FLOSS license. But this is only true where people can work together; licenses that make software impossible to recombine software into larger solutions (i.e., incompatible licenses) significantly reduces the effectiveness of FLOSS approaches.

Back in 1999, Bruce Perens warned of the problem, saying, "Do not write a new license if it is possible to use [an existing common license]. The propogation of many different and incompatible licenses works to the detriment of Open Source software because fragments of one program can not be used in another program with an incompatible license." By 2005, HP's Martin Fink pleaded for reduction in the number of FLOSS licenses. The Open Source Initiative (OSI)'s proliferation report, which was a response to this outcry, noted that people used the term "license proliferation" with at least three meanings:

  1. too many different licenses makes it difficult for licensors to choose [what license to use].
  2. some licenses do not play well together. "Some people use 'license proliferation' to refer to the fact that some open source licenses do not inter-operate well with other open source licenses... This comment generally came from larger companies."
  3. too many licenses makes it difficult to understand what you are agreeing to in a multi-license distribution. (e.g., "it takes a lot of time to read and understand them all").
In my mind, it is the second meaning that is the biggest problem, as suggested by Perens. In short, there is much less value to a FLOSS approach if no one can make further improvements in a project due to a forest of incompatible licenses. The third point is a variation on the second. This paper is hopefully a solution to the first point, the problem of choosing a license in the first place.

So why are there many odd, almost-never-used FLOSS licenses that have immediately died off, in spite of numerous calls by FLOSS experts to not create new licenses? I believe that one cause was that companies often called on their "intellectual property" (IP) lawyers when considering licensing a program under FLOSS. While these lawyers typically understand the legal underpinnings (e.g., copyright law), they typically did not fully understand what their clients were trying to do. In particular, I suspect that they often failed to understand the critical importance of working with a larger community of developers, including the ability to reuse others' code, to have that code be reused, and/or to be able to quickly understand the rights granted without needing to read dozens of legal licenses. Many lawyers are trained in how to create new contracts, and these lawyers were under the mistaken assumption that since they were releasing new code, they could simply write a tailored, unique license for each new project. Unfortunately, they failed to realize that these unique licenses, though possibly quite good in isolation, were incompatible with the existing wisely-used FLOSS licenses that cover software representing billions of dollars of effort. They also failed to realize that developers don't really want to read hundreds of different legal licenses and then figure out the consequences of merging them; instead, developers want a very few licenses so that such legal headaches are avoided. In many cases, no one even checked to see if it really met the Open Source Definition or the Free Software Definition. But even the list of licenses that did pass such scrutiny is very long. The predictable result was projects that languished because developers could - and did! - ignore the software that was released under these odd licenses, precisely because it used non-standard licensing.

In short, when releasing a new project, you should only consider using one of a very short list of mutually-compatible licenses that are widely used. This will enable you to combine that software later with other programs developed by others.

So, how can you derive that set of licenses? Several sites, such as Freshmeat and SourceForge, maintain statistics on FLOSS project licenses. Other studies such as my Gigabuck paper examine the number of lines of code in various programs actually in use. Their results tend to be quite similar, even though their sampling methods are different. According to Freshmeat, as of 2007-07-31 the top ten FLOSS licenses are:

In short, all surveys agree that the most popular FLOSS license by far is the GNU GPL - most FLOSS programs are released under the GNU GPL, and no other FLOSS license is even close to its popularity. This does not mean that you must choose the GNU GPL license, but it does meant that if you are wise, you will choose a license that is compatible with the GPL. The paper "Make Your Open Source Software GPL-Compatible. Or Else." explains in much more detail why GPL-compatibility is a necessary condition for a useful FLOSS license today, including many other quantitative studies.

So among the GPL-compatible licenses, how far should you look? Clearly, the list above of the most popular licenses is a reasonable starting point. The OSI proliferation report also identifies a very small set of "Licenses that are popular and widely used or with strong communities". In short, take lists of the most popular licenses (like those), extract the GPL-compatible licenses, and you have a good starting point.

The FLOSS licenses that meet this minimal criteria (the most popular licenses that are combinable with software that uses the most popular FLOSS license) are the MIT, BSD-new, LGPL, and GPL licenses. Historically the compatibility of the Apache 2.0 license with the GPL was debated; with the new GPL version 3 (GPLv3), there is no question, as it's universally acknowledged that the Apache 2.0 license is compatible with GPLv3. (The issue of the new version of the GPL and LGPL will be addressed later.) So there are at least reasons to consider using the Apache 2.0 license.

Do not release software to the public domain (that is, specifially disclaiming any copyright) unless you are a U.S. government employees who wrote the software as part of your official duties. Software in the public domain is compatible with all other FLOSS licenses, so from a compatibility perspective "public domain" is a fine choice. But there are some legal debates on whether or not it's even legally possible in the U.S. to release software to the public domain. In addition, releasing software to the public domain strips the developer of some legal protections against lawsuits that all popular FLOSS licenses provide. For U.S. government employees this is less of an issue, since the government can't be sued unless the government permits it (!), but it's a serious issue for anyone else. All lawyers who have seriously examined the issue have recommended using FLOSS licenses that include legal disclaimers. Releasing software under the MIT license is essentially the same as releasing to the public domain, but with additional legal protections, so it's much wiser to use the MIT license instead of releasing to the public domain.

Other relatively widely-used licenses have problems, including the Artistic and MPL licenses. Even back in 1999, Perens recommended against using the Artistic license because of clarity problems. The new Artistic license version 2.0 helps, but other licenses make sense to use unless you're making a library for Perl (a special community who typically use a GPL / Artistic dual license). The Mozilla Public License (MPL) was originally developed for Mozilla, but is GPL-incompatible; Mozilla found that this was such a mistake that they ended up relicensing their software under the MPL, LGPL, and GPL. Bruce Perens helped write the Netscape Public License (NPL), which the MPL is based on, and has this to say about his own creation: "Many companies have adopted a variation of the MPL for their own programs. This is unfortunate, because the NPL was designed for the specific business situation that Netscape was in at the time it was written, and is not necessarily appropriate for others to use. It should remain the license of Netscape and Mozilla, and others should use the GPL or the or X licenses."

Again, this is only discussing new projects. If you're modifying an existing program, and returning your modification back to the project, you'll normally need to release it with whatever license is used by the project. If your project must work with an existing project (e.g., it's a library for a language, or a plug-in), you should normally use the license common for that particular community... again, to maximize code reuse. So if you're making a library for Perl, you should normally license it under both the Artistic and GPL license, because that is a special community who typically use a GPL / Artistic dual license. But these are special cases.

So now we're down to a nice short list: MIT, BSD-new, LGPL, and GPL licenses (with versioning issues for LGPL and GPL)... and possibly Apache 2.0. Now let's examine what they mean, and how to select between them.

Gain understanding of those few licenses

Now that we've winnowed down the set of possible licenses to a few reasonable possibilities, we can examine them in more detail.

Unsurprisingly, the main licenses can be grouped into just a few categories, which reflect significant differences in their general goals. In particular, FLOSS licenses can be generally be grouped into "permissive" licenses or "protective" licenses. "Permissive" licenses permit the software to be released under non-FLOSS terms, while "protective" (aka copyleft) licenses protect the software from being released under non-FLOSS terms. There are, in turn, two main types of protective licenses; ones that only protect that particular module (but allow it to be included in a non-FLOSS program), and strongly protective licenses (which require that if the module is in a larger program, the entire program must be FLOSS).

Here's a simple matrix that summarizes this:
License Must receive source code of module modifications Entire Mixed result must be FLOSS
Permissive (MIT, BSD-new, Apache 2.0) no no
Weakly protective (LGPL) yes no
Strongly protective (GPL) yes no

Side-effects of each:

Permissive: _Code_ gets more widely used (incorporated into proprietary projects too), but the FLOSS _project_ risks always losing momentum because developers are not required to release changes back to project. Much higher risk of forking, esp. with proprietary branches (financial incentive to do so). Often ends up with many incompatible branches, many of them proprietary, which do not share code/improvements, and eventually branches cannot share code or improvements. Ex: BSD. This risk doesn't always happen, e.g., Apache project going strong. Permissive very useful in getting an idea or a specification in use.

Protective: FLOSS project stays FLOSS, because license requires it. Multiple companies may work on it together, because there's no financial incentive to forking. Thus, project more protected against forks, and strongly protected from proprietary forks... so more likely to have a strong project maintaining the software. Ex: GTK+ toolkit, Linux kernel. Weak copyleft (LGPL): Modifications of component stay FLOSS, but component users can be either both proprietary or FLOSS programs, so wider use than strong copyleft. Strong copyleft (GPL): Modifications or users of component must be GPL (essentially). For some, this is a problem; for others, it's an advantage, as discussed further below.

Pick a license based on your goals for that particular project

We've now found a small set of license candidates, and we've looked at their basic differences. But many guides stop here, and that's unfortunate. Just knowing about licenses for many people isn't enough; the question is, which license should you pick?

The best license for a project depends on your goals for that project. Different licenses support different kinds of maintenance approaches and business models. So, you should decide on your goal (including your business model), then map to licenses. Indeed, I personally have released different programs under different licenses (the MIT, LGPL, and GPL licenses, as well as proprietary) - because I had different goals for each one. Even the FSF, creator and a strong backer of the GPL, supports other licenses on occasion depending on their goals.

Let's first quickly dispel a myth, though. ??? Which license is "most business-friendly"? Answer: They all are. Each license is the most business-friendly, depending on your business model. Ref quotes.

Developing or trying to choose a "perfect" license that exactly meets your goals, but is non-standard (uncommon), is in the end a disaster. You don't want the code merely to exist; you want to encourage others to help improve and maintain it, so that the costs of doing so are distributed to others. Instead, it is better to pick a common and "good enough" license that enables you to build a large community. It's not hard to find analogies; vast effort has been spent to create common rulesets for commerce, including international agreements such as the "Berne Convention for the Protection of Literary and Artistic Works", and intra-national agreements such as the U.S. "Uniform Commercial Code" (UCC). Without common laws, commerce would be greatly impeded. Common FLOSS licenses have the same result; by having a few FLOSS licenses that are easily combined, uncertainty is greatly reduced. Reduced uncertainty results in more commerce, and thus, more wealth for everyone (and I mean wealth in its more general sense, including the set of goods and services available to you). If combining FLOSS code required the review of 50 different licenses to ensure compatibility, nothing worthwhile could be done.

Ideally, you'd consider a host of different business models, select one, and select the license that best supports it. Things are not quite cut-and-dried, indeed, there's research in this area. But new business models and markets are constantly being developed in other areas, too, and yet much has been learned that is still relevant. Conclusions

These are only my recommendations. This isn't legal advice (I'm not a lawyer), and in rare cases there are special circumstances that mean these aren't sufficient. Yet for most people, in most circumstances, I believe following these recommendations will give you the best results, and I hope that you will find them helpful.

    In chapter 7, he notes that "Depending on the license, the developer may or may not have the ability to choose a different license to apply to the new project. If that pre-existing project uses an MIT or BSD-type license, the developer can use virtually any license, so long as the proper notification and disclaimer provisions are included. On the other hand, if the pre-existing project were licensed under the GPL, the developer would have little choice but to license his or her own project under the GPL or to get permission from the author to use a different license... in many situations, a developer’s choice of license is constrained by choices made by his predecessors.

    In those situations in which a developer is in fact starting from scratch or from code whose license is amenable to change, the decision as to license will probably be largely a matter of personal preference. The factors that might influence this deci- sion include: how frequently used and well-known the license is; how readily comprehensible that license is; and finally, and perhaps most importantly, the license’s philosophy, and, in particular, the extent to which the license allows with code developed under other licenses, including proprietary licenses.

    "In choosing any license to apply to a new project, developers should strongly consider relying on those licenses already well-known in the development community. This makes the project much more transparent to other developers and potential contributors who will probably have a better grasp of the principles of the BSD, MIT, Apache, MPL, and GPL Licenses, than they would of the Monongahela Copper Mining Institute Database License, v8.3. To the extent that licensing issues are important to contributors, using a license already known to them reduces barriers to entry and will likely make for a more successful project. For much the same reason, using licenses that are written more clearly and which do not contain ambiguous or unusual terms will also help a project succeed. The BSD and MIT Licenses are models in this regard. The Apache License Version 1.1 is both clear and well-known in the development community, and Version 2.0 is becoming more familiar. The GPL and MPL Licenses, while considerably more complex, are well-written and their principles are well-understood. Developers should avoid licenses that seem ambiguous, unduly confusing, or poorly written. The most important decision in choosing a license will be the choice between a GPL License and a less restrictive license. A full discussion of the disagreement between the two camps is beyond the scope of this book. However, to put it briefly, the GPL is premised on the belief that non-free software is to be avoided and that free software development projects should be set up to encourage open development models of software development and to discourage reliance on software not developed under an open development model, including all proprietary-licensed software. By requiring that any code developed from or based on GPL-licensed code be GPL-licensed, the GPL creates a strong incentive for programmers to license their code under a GPL License, in the form of access to all the code already GPL-licensed. The argument in favor of less-restrictive licenses—such as the MIT, BSD, Apache, and MPL Licenses — is that open development model of software development is not inconsistent with the development of software under other models, including proprietary models. The fact that one line of a program, such as the Sendmail program described in Chapter 2, is developed under a proprietary license, does not undermine the open development model, in this view. The more developers and users that are involved in working on particular code, the better, even if some of that development takes place in “closed” development models under proprietary licenses. In sum, there is no ready answer as to which license is the best for a given project. While a certain license may be better suited for a project, particularly when a substantial amount of work has already been done under that license, such decisions depend largely on circumstances and on the taste of the project developer.

    As should be evident from the previous discussion, drafting a new open source license is probably not the best place to start for most open source projects. In addition to the extra time and expense associated with drafting any legal document, the use of a new license will discourage potential contributors from participating in the project. Those contributors who are concerned about licensing implications will want to read and understand the license. Particularly in the case of long or complex licenses, this may present a substantial barrier to entry."

    Chapter 7 also notes that, "by permitting non-open development of code developed under them, code licensed under the MIT or the BSD License may be more prone to forking than code licensed under other licenses."

    He has three criteria:
    • [Must] Maintain Attribution and Disclaimer
    • Must Distribute Modified Source [when distribute binary]
    • [Must use] Same License for Larger Work [it is embedded in]
    He believes that "Other Open Source licenses have been excluded because I believe that they have been superannuated by modern licenses such as the AFL, CPL, and the OSL. The GPL and LGPL may fit into this category but I included them because they are widely used."

    Unfortunately, this view completely fails to take into account the large body of existing code and projects, and the license compatibility problem such license changes cause. In practice, the AFL, CPL, and OSL are rarely used compared to the GPL or LGPL, and there is no evidence that they are significantly gaining in support... but these facts don't strongly impact the article. Thus, while the analysis is valid, and the questions asked in particular are valuable, I do not recommend using this list as a "recommended" list of licenses. But these licenses are reasonable representatives of permissive (BSD-new, MIT, AFL), weak copyleft (LGPL, CPL), and strong copyleft (GPL, OSL) licenses, so using them as a starting point for thinking about the issues is a good idea.

  • Evaluation of Public Software Licenses by Donald K. Rosenberg (Stromian Technologies). This old document suggests that the lowest levels (e.g., operating system) tend to be GPLed, while the higher layers (e.g., toolkits and applications) will be split between proprietary and non-proprietary licenses. He specifically examines the issue of forking, suggesting that permissive licenses (e.g., BSD-new) are far more likely to fork than GPL'ed software.
  • Open Source Licensing: Software Freedom and Intellectual Property Law by Lawrence E. Rosen (book). (ISBN 0-13-148787-6). This book was discussed on Slashdot. and an interview with Rosen is at "Choosing an open source license" by Mary E. Tyler, NewsForge, July 26, 2004. Lawrence E. Rosen is general counsel of the Open Source Initiative (OSI), so not surprisingly he's very knowledgeable.

    Rosen's book is a general book about FLOSS licensing, and well worth reading. It's a little old (much has happened since), as a lawyer he goes into depth that many people won't want, and he takes specific positions that in several cases are debatable; but if you want some depth in analysis of the legal issues, this is a good place to start. Here's a list of the book sections: Freedom and Open Source; Intellectual Property; Distribution of Software; Taxonomy of Licenses; Academic Licenses [meaning licenses such as MIT and BSD-new]; Reciprocity and the GPL; The Mozilla Public License (MPL); The Common Public License (CPL); The OSL and the AFL; Choosing an Open Source License; Shared Source, Eventual Source, and Other Licensing Models; Open Source Litigation; and Open Standards; it concludes with a non-chapter labelled The Open Source Paradigm.

    This book's chapter 10 is specifically on "Choosing an Open Source License". He first discusses free-riders - where licensees reap benefits of others’ work with no return obligations. Open source software licenses can't completely halt free-riding, though reciprocal (aka protective or copylefting) licenses have the advantage of requiring free-riding to be equally shared by all distributors of derivative works (not just by the original licensor). Instead, he notes that most in the FLOSS community believe that the "growing commons of open source software that generally results from open source licensing is... sufﬁcient reward for allowing everyone to be free-riders."

    Regarding the question "How can we make money from distributing this software under an open source license?", he first notes that business models that rely on selling software at high per-copy prices don't work well, because FLOSS licenses allow anyone to become a distributor and compete on price, driving the per-copy price down. But of course, this is not the only possible development model; commercial companies "can make money on what the open source license doesn’t grant. For this reason, it is often more rewarding to consider the exclusions from license rather than the open source grants of license when looking for opportunities for proﬁt. The most important exclusion is trademark or brand identity. Trademarks are excluded from all open source licenses, either explicitly or implicitly... Despite their protestations that quality matters most, companies and individuals usually acquire software not by function but by reputation. Trademarks are thus very important factors in consumer decisions... By marketing software under a trademark, the licensor can sell perceived value... customers are often willing to pay for brand-name software, particularly if it comes with support and other beneﬁts... Also excluded from several open source licenses, for many of the same reasons, are the names and reputations of the licensors... Most warranties are also excluded from the open source licenses in this book. A separate business can be made from selling such warranties — as well as other support and installation services - separate from the software itself. Finally, the licensor of open source software is always free to license his or her software also under other terms and condi- tions [aka dual licensing]."

    Rosen pleads: "If at all possible, licensors should use an existing template license. Please don’t invent your own. The open source community is not seeking new licenses to analyze and interpret. The proliferation of open source licenses creates a serious problem: It risks additional fragmentation of the public commons of free software. While software under some academic licenses can be combined without restriction, combining soft- ware under different reciprocal licenses — particularly the more complex reciprocal licenses used by large companies — requires that lawyers or skilled licensing professionals review each of the licenses for incompatibilities. Even where the differences between licenses are trivial, such as their designation of governing law, a combinatorial analysis of open source licenses rapidly becomes a nontrivial exercise... Without exception, leaders of the open source community discourage the submission of 'yet another license.' Any software company deciding to distribute its software under an open source license is fervently encouraged to select among the existing licenses rather than to create a new one."

    I should make a few comments about this book, esp. regarding some of the specific positions he takes. Rosen states that while the FSF believes the Apache and GPLv2 licenses are incompatible, Rosen believes they are compatible. This view is probably less widely held nowadays, especially given the special additional changes to make GPLv3 compatible with the Apache 2.0 license, and when Rosen claims it's just about trademarks I believe he is in error. Note that he uses the term "Academic license" instead of "permissive", which I find a little misleading; many academic organizations use other kinds of licenses, and many non-academic organizations use permissive licenses. His term "reciprocal" is different than the term "protective" used by many others, but it's a very good term.

    The text above notes the many problems of creating new licenses, but elsewhere he lists a long list of legal questions to consider about licenses. That's fine, but beware of being led by those questions into creating a new license anyway; examining all issues can be valuable, but creating a "perfect" new license will almost certainly be worse than picking a "close enough" license that enables you to build a community and reuse existing code. Indeed, he is himself the creator of "yet another license"; he created the "Academic Free License" (AFL), which he likes (naturally). Just considering things from a legal point-of-view, the AFL is a fine license, but that is not enough. The problem is, the AFL is not a popular license, and in particular versions 1.2 and 2.1 are GPL-incompatible (a terrible idea), and version 3.0's GPL-compatibility is not yet known (but I expect that it's incompatible too). I believe the AFL should never be used, if at all possible, until its compatibility with the GPL is assured; its use is just too marginal to justify making yourself incompatible with most FLOSS programs in most cases.

    Rosen also has very interesting papers on Trademark and OSS and Fair use.
