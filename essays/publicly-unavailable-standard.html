Publicly Unavailable Standard (PUS)

David A. Wheeler

2025-09-19

For purposes of this document, a "Publicly Unavailable Specification" or "Publicly Unavailable Standard" (PUS) is a specification or standard that can't be freely and immediately viewed and applied by anyone by using the internet and a URL, typically because there's a required fee, or it requires registration, or it requires special extraction tools (so it can't be viewed on mobile devices). This is in contrast with a "Freely Available Specification" or "Freely Available Standard" (FAS); for purposes of this document a FAS is a specification or standard that can be freely and immediately viewed and applied by anyone by using the internet and a URL, without restriction like fees or registration or special extraction tools. You'll also see the term "Free standard" or "libre standard" or "public standard" in use.

Historically standardization required people to accept PUSs. That's because distribution of standards required the use of a printing press and experienced people to set the type. Old standards organizations like ISO and IEC long predate the Internet, and they needed this mechanisms. However, now we have the Internet, yet we still have legacy pre-Internet processes that release PUSs.

Many standards bodies already exclusively release FASs as defined here, meaning, available to all without fees, registrations, or other restrictions. This includes the IETF (who define the internet standards), OASIS, W3C, and many more.

Unfortunately, some still use the legacy PUS process to release standards. That's a problem, since that means most people who are supposed to be applying the standards cannot practically read or use them. If there was only one standard, it wouldn't be so bad, but modern society requires an endless number of standards.

What's worse, PUS tends to spread. One PUS is rarely enough, because a PUS usually cites other PUSs, which cite other PUSs. These create a web of PUSs and ensure that most potential users cannot afford to see or use them. PUS tends to create two worlds, a "haves" world who can see the PUS, and another group who cannot. Some in that second group cope by ignoring copyright. However, requiring people to disobey the law as a practical matter is not something anyone should condone.

Some ask "how can you fund standardization?" - a question I find amusing. First, most people who develop the standards are typically not paid by the standards bodies, so what's most important is already paid for by other means. Many standards bodies like the IETF have been developing standards for decades without any of those standards requiring the release of PUS, and all standards bodies freely release some standards. In addition, standards and specifications are basically laws in modern society. Societal laws should not be secret and visible only to those who have the money to afford them. Laws that people are subject to should be available to all, without restriction. Governments and large organizations already pay for the work to develop many standards; they shouldn't need to pay twice.

I should note that many use the term "publicly available standard" to mean a freely available standard (e.g., historically ISO). I don't use that term here, because there's been a lot of effort to redefine the term PAS. I suspect one reason for this push is that this older definition of PAS implicitly admits that a PUS is not publicly available. To avoid confusion, I'm using different terms.

I'm very pro-standards. Standards are critically necessary for today's modern world. We need standards organizations like ISO and IETF. We should profusely thank them for the hard work they've done that's been foundational for modern society. However, in the long term we need to help these important organizations adapt to modern times. The problem is PUS. We now have an Internet. We don't need any more PUS.
