Data Rights are More Important than Requirements

David A. Wheeler

2015-12-08

If you are in the government, and acquiring custom developed software, I hope to convince you of this truth: data rights are more important than requirements.

The problem with requirements

Of course, meeting the actual needs of users over time is paramount, since that is the whole point of developing software for others. However, do not make the mistake of thinking that requirements are the same thing as actual user needs. By "requirements" I mean a recorded representation that attempts to identify the current user needs. A map is not the territory, and for the same reason, requirements are not the actual user needs. Requirements are just a current best guess of user needs, written down so people can review and refine them.

Yes, it's important to identify the requirements for custom software. But there are key limitations of any written requirements, for example:

  1. Requirements never exactly and perfectly capture the current user needs. Requirements in practice always omit key needs and include requirements that are not real needs. After decades of observation I've never seen requirements precisely capture the actual user needs.
  2. Even if they were perfect, requirements are never understood in exactly the same way by everyone. Pictures, prototypes, and mathematical specification languages can help, but they are no guarantee.
  3. Things always change. Even if your requirements were perfect yesterday, today the environment will change, or the user perceived needs will change.

An acquisition process that presumes that requirements are perfect, universally understood, and will never change is typically a disaster. Identifying requirements is important, yes, but it is far more important to be able to make changes into the future.

Data rights are power

Data rights, aka intellectual rights or technical data rights, are the set of rights that different parties have, including the rights to make changes and share them with others. A common mistake is thinking that data rights are just legal or technical details. They are not.

Data rights are another word for power. Data rights determine who has power, and who does not.

Data rights are more important than requirements, because adequate data rights are the necessary legal condition for supporting requirements changes. And as we established earlier, requirements always need to change, for variety of reasons including error, misunderstandings, and changing situations.

There are some in government to understand software issues, & I am honored to have worked with some of them. Unfortunately, a vast number of people who are charged with managing software acquisition simply do not understand software. As a result, they make disastrous decisions that are difficult to recover from. For example they failed to retain unlimited rides for the government that it normally would keep. Cannot compete, cannot change later. It is critically important to be able to compete. Building software is not at all like building a device. It is like building a city. Once you have completed initial construction, you will live in it for decades into the future. You only learn what you need next by living in it. Whoever has the data rights has the power to make whatever changes are necessary to that city you are living in. If you do not have the data right you are not living in the city, you are living in a prison cell. You cannot change anything, and you cannot escape it. The y2k problem showed why thinking that hardware and software are the same as such a big mistake. People thought that software last of the same length of time at the hardware it was developed for. That's completely nonsense. Hardware goes away when there's better hardware or the hardware works out. Software goes away when the problem disappears or when you can no longer maintain it. If you have the necessary rights, you can transition it to newer technology and continue to maintain it for your use. Open source software. Esau and Jacob. DFARS 7014 better; it only requires you to determine who paid. FAR clause requires you to predict future (you're kidding!). You always need to consider exit costs. There issues when using off the shelf software, but it's not quite the same thing. The exit cost is usually transitioning to another alternative software. But with custom software, usually the only real alternative is to rebuild it. This creates a much larger exit cost.
