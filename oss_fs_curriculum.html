Open Source Software / Free Software (OSS/FS) Curriculum

David A. Wheeler
dwheeler@dwheeler.com
Revised as of January 14, 2003

This is an early draft of curriculum for a course in open source software / free software (OSS/FS). This course is intended for college/university students, at the junior/senior level or graduate school, as an elective. It's presumed that the students are already familiar with general software development issues, and at least have a basic understanding of how proprietary software is developed and sold.

Here's a course description:

This course will explore the Open Source Software / Free Software (OSS/FS) movement from the historical, legal, economic, public policy, and technical perspectives to gain a better understanding of it. OSS/FS is changing the software industry and we will look at why and how. Part of each class will discuss topics from the pages of newspapers and trade magazines to place the issues in the context of the course.

The numbers indicate order, not a "class number", in some cases several could be combined into a single class. When in doubt, I've left a topic in, because it's easier to remove than to add - but that means there's probably too much here for one semester.

Textbook: "Open Sources: Voices from the Open Source Revolution". Edited by Chris deBona, Sam Ockman and Mark Stone. 1st Edition January 1999. O'Reilly. ISBN 1-56592-582-3. http://www.oreilly.com/catalog/opensources/book/toc.html.

  1. Introduction. This would include definitions and be an overview of the topics to follow. Definitions: OSI's "open source", FSF's "Free Software". Alternative terms: Libre Software, Free-libre software, OSS/FS, FLOSS, FOSS. Antonyms: proprietary code, closed source. Note that OSS can be commercial, or not; the term "commercial" is not an antonym to OSS/FS. Contrast motivations of "Open Source Software" and "Free Software" Discuss basic development approaches, similarity to scientific method and academic publication. Motivate studying by giving evidence of value (e.g., significant market shares in various fields). Discuss:
    • Key Timeline/history: Early software, Stallman/Free Software Foundation, GNU, emacs, BSD, Linux kernel, "Cathedral and Bazaar", Netscape, term "open source" invented, Halloween documents 1 and 2, Oracle, IBM support. See the LWN timelines for 1998+.
    • Key products/exemplars: Linux, Apache, Sendmail, bind, ...
    • Key people: Richard Stallman, Eric S. Raymond, Bruce Perens, Linus Torvalds, ...
    • Key Linux distributions: Red Hat, Debian, Novell/SuSE, MandrakeSoft, ...
    • Other key things/projects: Sourceforge, Freshmeat.net, Linux Weekly News, Slashdot.
    • Licenses: GPL, LGPL, BSD, MIT, MPL, ... Contrast GPL vs. MIT ("Whose freedom?")
    • Key phrases ("Free as in speech, not free as in beer").

    Readings:

    1. Open Sources/introduction [Chris DiBona, Sam Ockman, and Mark Stone; in Voices]
    2. OSS/FS References http://dwheeler.com/oss_fs_refs.html

  2. History/Background. Timeline/history: Early software, Stallman/Free Software Foundation, GNU, emacs, BSD, Linux kernel, "Cathedral and Bazaar", Netscape, term "open source" invented, Halloween documents 1 and 2, Oracle, IBM support. See the LWN timelines for 1998+. Discuss RMS's motivations, vs. BSD's. Is it GNU/Linux or Linux operating system? Discuss key participants/opponents (RMS, ESR, Perens, Torvalds, Brian Behlendorf, Bill Gates (inc. "Open Letter to Hobbyists")). Key organizations: Free Software Foundation (FSF), OSI, IBM, HP, Microsoft (as user and as opposer), ... Key projects: Linux kernel, *BSDs, Apache, gcc, ... Key web sites: www.slashdot.org, lwn.net, freshmeat.net, sourceforge, ... Go through project case studies. Discuss the "top 10" projects. Briefly discuss top 50 (makes no sense to get through a course without knowing a one-line summary of the most important projects).

  3. Legal and License Issues. Basics of Intellectual Property law: copyright, patent, trade secret. DMCA. In-depth analysis of common OSS licenses, e.g., what EXACTLY does the GPL say; LGPL, BSD, MIT, MPL, Artistic? (17 USC 101: The term ''financial gain'' includes receipt, or expectation of receipt, of anything of value, including the receipt of other copyrighted works.) (patent cross-licensing. GPL as copyright-enforced cross-licensing of code) Copyleft vs. non-copyleft. GPL-compatible vs. non-GPL-compatible. Patent defense approaches. Dual licenses. GPL is a license, not a contract; U.S. law differences. Compare to proprietary EULAs. Examine edge cases: subclassing in LGPL. Proprietary Linux kernel modules, derived works in copyright law. Major case law studies. Software Patents - what is the controversy? Case studies/examples: SCO vs. IBM, GPL enforcement actions by the FSF (see Eben Moglen's article on enforcing the GPL)

    Readings:

    1. The Open Source Definition [Bruce Perens, in Voices]
    2. GPL text [FSF]
    3. GPL FAQ [FSF]
    4. LGPL [FSF]
    5. MIT/X11 license
    6. BSD-old and BSD-new license
    7. U.S. Code on copyright law: 17 USC (esp. intro and major concepts) (maybe patent law: 35 USC, trademark law, contract law). US Code available at: http://www4.law.cornell.edu/uscode (copyright law also posted at http://www.copyright.gov/title17/92chap5.html)
    8. GPL is a License, not a Contract http://lwn.net/Articles/60455/

  4. Business/Economic Issues and Motivations Reasons to consider using OSS/FS, reasons why organizations use OSS/FS. Reasons why businesses support developing OSS/FS (include Case studies/examples: Apache, Linux, g++, ...). Developer Motivations: discuss "Homesteading the Noosphere". Examine developer/business surveys such as Boston Consulting Group/OSDN's http://www.osdn.com/bcg and the FLOSS study http://www.infonomics.nl/FLOSS. Discuss reasons users might contribute (want functionality, avoid costs of maintaining their own branch).

    Readings:

    1. Why OSS/FS? Look at the Numbers! http://dwheeler.com/oss_fs_why.html
    2. "The Magic Cauldron" [ESR]
    3. "Homesteading the Noosphere" [ESR]
    4. "Giving it away: How Red Hat Software stumbled across a new economic model and helped improve an industry" [Robert Young, in Voices]
    5. "Future of Cygnus Systems: An Entrepreneur's Account" [Michael Tiemann, in Voices]
    6. "Open Source as a Business Strategy" [Brian Behlendorf, in Voices]

  5. Governmental/public policy issues. Potential government responses, and their reasons. Equal footing: saving money, increasing options (see U.S. DoD). Encourage: increase industrial base, reduce dependence on outsiders, increase security/safety, reduce "digital divide", reduce piracy (WTO precondition) (See China, Japan). Issues: is governmental funding of an OSS/FS like paying a proprietary vendor (e.g., like a site license)? GPL'ing research results; requiring/default all research results be licenses under an OSS/FS license, etc. Policies that impede OSS/FS (DMCA, software patents). Polices enable competition or OSS/FS (interoperability, preferring open standards). Open Source Software Institute (John Weathersby), Project Leopard (leopard.sourceforge.net).

    Readings:

    1. Report for Congress, Computer Software and Open Source Issues: A primer [Congressional Research Service],
    2. Developing Open Source Software to Advance High End Computing [President's Information Technology Advisory Committee]
    3. (second) MITRE report on DoD use of OSS/FS, Bollinger et al.

  6. Education and Research. Using OSS/FS in education to lower costs. Teaching software engineering and implementation concepts through OSS/FS code (e.g., teaching operating systems by studying GNU/Linux code).

    OSS/FS code as a facilitator/basis for research (OSS/FS lowering barriers to the cost of changes). Releasing OSS/FS as "executable publication" of results. Software engineering research through analysis of production OSS/FS code and development processes. Concerns that businesses or researchers may not improve/release OSS/FS code since they may receive less money for it. Issues involving release of code from research as GPL'd software. Issues involving permitting research code to be made only proprietary.

    Readings:

    1. "More than a Gigabuck" http://dwheeler.com/sloc

  7. Security. Discuss issues raised about OSS/FS security by proprietary vendors. The source code is visible to all (attacker and defender), what's the impact? Contrast with proprietary vendors.

    Readings:

    1. Secure Programming for Linux and Unix HOWTO (section on open source's impact). http://dwheeler.com/secure-programs

  8. Issues Raised about OSS/FS. Discuss issues raised about OSS/FS by proprietary vendors: innovation, support, user friendliness, .... Discuss pros and cons, and in particular examine evidence not just opinions.

    Discuss the pros and cons of competition, using as examples GNOME/GTK+ vs. KDE/Qt, vi(m) vs. emacs.

    Readings:

    1. "How to evaluate OSS/FS" http://dwheeler.com/oss_fs_eval.html

  9. OSS Development Processes and Models. General model: developer->trusted developer->project->distributor->user "Cathedral vs. Bazaar" (discuss general concept and Raymond's paper)

    Development approaches: one-developer, small-number-of-developers-using-CVS (Sourceforce/CVS), Linux kernel (hierarchical, bake-offs/competing branches, handoff to stable (with new head), even/odd version numbering), Apache (voting quorum: X agree, no veto)

    Forks: What they are, their dangers, why the ability to fork is considered necessary. Discuss impact of licenses (e.g., GPL). Discuss well-known forks (glibc vs. libc, gcc vs. egcs); ongoing (XFree86)

    Controversy on using proprietary tools (e.g., BitKeeper, Java Virtual Machine) and/or those subject to proprietary vendor's whims (e.g., C# reimplementations).

    Readings:

    1. "The Cathedral and the Bazaar" essay [ESR]
    2. Software Release Practice HOWTO
    3. The Rise of ``Worse is Better'' by Richard Gabriel

  10. OSS Design Concepts. Why concepts from Unix and TCP/IP are heavily intertwined. Thinking in Unix: "Keep it simple stupid". Breaking things down into small parts that do one thing well. Program generators as a key concept.

    Layering concepts: Key libraries/infrastructure, how components layered: kernel, C library (glibc), language library, other libraries, application. GUIs: X and X protocol, xlib, widget library (GTK+/Qt), desktop environment libraries (GNOME/KDE environment libraries). Example: How a Linux OS starts up.

    Readings:

    1. "Art of Unix..." [ESR], initial chapter (this summarizes the whole book).

  11. Trying out an OSS/FS system. Try Knoppix, etc. Work with at least a few examples. Or install Debian and then try it out. Try several different uses: Web server, office/desktop.

  12. OSS Technology. Coordination techniques (and their pitfalls): mailing lists, bug trackers (note Bugzilla), email, chat (note IRC), project conferences Briefly discuss common tools:
    1. Configuration Management tools: Why CM tools are critical; diff/patch (Larry Wall) and their importance; Non-locking CM tools (esp. CVS) and importance to OSS
    2. Build system: make and friends, autoconf.
    3. Editors: vim, emacs, and the ancient vi/emacs rivalry.
    4. Development Languages. http://dwheeler.com/sloc for Language usage analysis. Compare major languages: C, C++, shell (including ksh), Lisp, assembly, Perl, Fortran, Python, tcl, Java, yacc/bison, expect, lex/flex, (awk, Objective-C, Ada, C shell, Pascal, and sed).
    5. gcc suite.
    6. Source debuggers.
    7. Program libraries: importance. Static/shared/dynamic libraries. Impact of library licenses (GPL vs. LGPL vs. MIT).
    8. Portable GUIs: wxWindows, Mozilla, etc. Web browser as universal interface
    9. IDEs.
    10. Program generators, multi-lingual approaches. Why "make" and "primitive text techniques" continue to thrive when developing larger programs instead of IDEs.
    11. Source distribution: tarballs. The standard "./configure && make && make install".
    12. Package managers (compare deb and rpm, Debian and Red Hat's).

    Discuss The impact of potentially having ALL code (including libraries) available when considering alternatives, performance monitoring, debugging.

    Linux distributions: how each are created/built. Use Debian and Red Hat as two contrasting examples. Discuss Debian "stable" vs. "unstable", organizational structure, rules/policies. Distributed package development. Discuss Red Hat, Red Hat CDs, how it creates its packages. Comparing their results.

  13. Standards. Importance of standards. Impact of OSS on standards (e.g., as "reference implementation"). Impact of standards on OSS (e.g, generally implement, submarine patents). Major organizations, how their policies relate to OSS: ISO, IEEE, W3C, IETF. Major standards: POSIX, ISO C, TCP/IP, HTTP, HTML. Linux Standard Base (LSB). Freedesktop.org.

    Readings:

    1. "The Internet Engineering Task Force" [Scott Brader, in Voices]
    2. Jim Gettys' Open Source Desktop Technology Road Map

  14. Applying OSS concepts outside software. Some OSS/FS advocates have stated that OSS/FS may not apply elsewhere, and that if it fails in other fields that doesn't mean it fails for software. Discuss Wikipedia/Wikimedia. See the New Scientist article The Great Giveaway by Graham Lawton, which discusses OpenCola, the Open Audio License (OAL), Wikipedia, and the OpenLaw project (at the Berkman Center for Internet and Society at Harvard Law School). The article makes a minor mistake: the GPL is the most common OSS/FS license, but it's not the only such license.. but that mistake doesn't really detract from the point of the article. The Creative Commons group has developed a set of licenses for written material of any kind, many of them clearly based on OSS/FS concepts (i.e., the ones that allow modifications and commercial uses of the work). Also discuss efforts in scientific journals to ensure scientific works are freely available to the public (or at least other scientists). Discuss aspects of a work that might suggest OSS/FS approaches would work, or would not work.

Supplemental Readings: Daily perusal of stories at Slashdot.org, Newsforge.com, LinuxToday.com, and LWN.net for in-class discussion.

Students should write a paper or give a presentation on a related topic. Examples:

  1. Examine an existing OSS/FS project in-depth (e.g., what it does, its licensing approach, its history, who participates in development, who uses it, examining its design, hands-on evaluation, what are its strengths and weaknesses, how does it compare to its competitors, what is your prognosis for its future).
  2. Examine/discuss developer or firm motivations for developing or using OSS/FS (or, choosing not to). Are they justified?
  3. Another might be to examine in more depth a general topic, such as a specific legal issue (enforceability of GPL, what is a "derived work"), proposed government policy, and so on.
  4. Compare and contrast OSS/FS licenses, and examine some unusual ones. Why have they not caught on?
  5. What are the challenges of OSS/FS? Identify them, and discuss how they might (or might not be) overcome.
